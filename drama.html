Configuration matrix

A configuration matrix is a table visualizing the presence and absence of characters throughout the course of a play. The scenes are entered on the x-axis and the speakers on the y-axis. A cell of the matrix is filled in when a speaker makes an appearance in a scene.

The configuration matrix is enriched by numerous drama-, act- and scene-level information like the configuration density, speech statistics and metrics concerning the speakers. This information can be viewed by hovering over the various cells of the matrix.

Content: Configuration matrix, various drama-, act-, scene- and speaker-statistics.

Structual analysis

Via the structural analysis you can examine various quantitative metrics and the development throughout the play, the acts and the scenes.

Available metrics:
Number of speakers, number of scenes, number of speeches, average speech length, median speech lengths, maximum speech length, minimum speech length (each in number of words).

Content: Act-scene-table, Bar chart - Acts statistics, Bar chart - Scenes statistics

Speaker analysis

With the speaker analysis, you can examine statistics and metrics about specific speakers, thus the characters of the plays. Furthermore, you can compare speakers with each other.

Available metrics:
Presence in scenes, presence per scene in percent, number of speeches, average speech lenght, median speech length, maximum speech length, minimum speech length (each in number of words).

A pie chart visualize die dominance of speech concerning the characters, thus which characters talk the most. Furthermore, you can analyze the scenic relations between speakers (dominant, concomitant, alternative and independent).
The calculation of scenic relations goes as follows: A is the set of numbers defining the scenes in which a character is present, e.g. A={1,4,5} says that the character is present in scene 1, 4 and 5. Comparing the sets of different characters leads to the following definitions:

Concomitant: The sets of two characters are the same.
Dominant: X is dominant to Y if the set of X contains all numbers of Y.
Independent: The sets of two characters are neither concomitant nor dominant meaning the characters meet each other in some scenes but are also seperated from each other in other scenes.
Alternative: The sets of two characters contain no similar numbers. The characters never meet each other.

Content: Speaker-table, Pie chart - dominance of speeches, Bar chart - Speaker statistics, Speaker relations

Distribution of speeches

The distribution of speeches visualizes the distribution of all occurring speech lengths. Speech lengths are calculated via number of words.

The distribution is presented as an interactive histogram and curve diagram. In all diagrams, all speeches are individually viewable. The distribution can be regarded as absolute value or as relative values in percentage.

Content: Distribution of speeches - Histogram, Distribution of speeches - Curve diagram
